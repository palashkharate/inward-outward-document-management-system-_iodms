# Chapter 2: Literature Review

This chapter provides a comprehensive review of contemporary research on Artificial Intelligence-based predictive maintenance for aircraft engine health monitoring. The literature surveyed encompasses various machine learning and deep learning approaches, their applications, and the challenges encountered in this critical domain. Each reviewed paper contributes unique insights into methodologies, datasets, and performance metrics, collectively advancing the state of the art in prognostic and health management for aviation.

## 2.1 Overview of Reviewed Literature

### 2.1.1 A Data-Driven Approach to Aircraft Engine MRO Using Enhanced ANNs Based on FMECA

Dagal et al. (2025) introduced a hybrid predictive maintenance framework that integrates Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) with Failure Modes, Effects, and Criticality Analysis (FMECA) [1]. The primary objective of this study was to enhance maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) efficiency by prioritizing critical failures and optimizing operations. The authors addressed the limitations of traditional maintenance methods, which often lead to excessive downtime and high costs, and highlighted the lack of systematic integration between AI-driven predictive maintenance and reliability assessment frameworks like FMECA. Their methodology involved training an ANN with historical engine sensor data (temperature, pressure, vibration, and oil analysis) to predict failure probabilities, repair durations, and costs. FMECA, utilizing the Risk Priority Number (RPN), was then employed to rank failures by severity, ensuring that the most critical issues received immediate attention. Additionally, Weibull distribution analysis was used to model component reliability and confirm wear-out failure modes. Validated with real aircraft engine data, the ANN-FMECA model achieved a 94.3% accuracy in failure prediction, improved maintenance prioritization efficiency by 15.7%, and reduced maintenance costs by 35.3% and unplanned outages by 40.5%. This approach significantly enhanced fleet availability, flight safety, and environmental impact. While the integration of AI with reliability engineering was a significant advantage, the authors acknowledged the persistent challenge of explainability and decision-making assistance in AI models, identifying this as a key research gap [1].

### 2.1.2 A Deep Learning Model for Remaining Useful Life Prediction of Aircraft Turbofan Engine on C-MAPSS Dataset

Asif et al. (2022) proposed an enhanced Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network, combined with effective pre-processing steps, for accurate Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction of aircraft turbofan engines using the NASA C-MAPSS dataset [2]. The study aimed to overcome the limitations of standalone LSTM networks, such as susceptibility to outliers, noise, un-normalized data, and un-correlated sensor values, which can hinder prediction accuracy. A novel piecewise linear degradation model was introduced to accurately determine the starting point of engine deterioration and assign RUL target labels. Sensor data underwent correlation analysis to select features with monotonous behavior relative to RUL, followed by filtering with a moving median filter and normalization. The deep LSTM network, trained with this meticulously pre-processed data and combined with dimensionality reduction, demonstrated improved RUL prediction, achieving minimum root mean squared error (RMSE) and score function values across all four C-MAPSS sub-datasets compared to existing methods. The primary advantage of this work lies in its emphasis on comprehensive data pre-processing to unlock the full potential of deep learning models for RUL prediction, addressing the research gap concerning the dependency of deep learning effectiveness on robust data preparation [2].

### 2.1.3 STARNet: Stacked Transfer-Aware for Robust Remaining Useful Life Prediction for C-MAPSS Multi-Regime Engines

Kapoor et al. (2026) introduced STARNet, a robust RUL prediction framework designed to address the challenge of generalization across diverse operating conditions and fault modes in C-MAPSS multi-regime engines without requiring retraining [3]. The authors identified that existing deep learning models often struggle to generalize beyond their training datasets, and traditional methods rely heavily on expert-designed features. STARNet employs a multi-scale Conv–BiLSTM architecture with attention pooling and window statistics fusion to capture statistical deterioration trends, long-range sequence dynamics, and local temporal dependencies. Robustness was further enhanced through phase-aware normalization with delta augmentation to stabilize sensor data distributions and the use of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) and Monte Carlo (MC) dropout inference for uncertainty-aware predictions. To improve generalization and efficiency, the framework incorporated self-supervised pretraining and targeted transfer learning between C-MAPSS subsets (e.g., FD002/FD004), alongside a multi-seed ensemble with nonlinear stacking for stable and accurate predictions. STARNet demonstrated steady performance gains across all C-MAPSS subsets, with average RMSE and score improvements of 5.91% and 13.23%, respectively, over existing state-of-the-art models. This work significantly contributes to developing operationally viable PdM systems capable of adapting across industrial environments, addressing the research gap of model generalization and robustness [3].

### 2.1.4 Using Federated Machine Learning in Predictive Maintenance of Jet Engines

Al Hasib et al. (2024) explored the application of federated machine learning (FL) for predicting the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of turbine jet engines, primarily focusing on addressing data privacy and security concerns while optimizing maintenance schedules [4]. The problem statement highlighted the challenges of data scarcity in small fleets and the unwillingness of large fleets to share sensitive operational data due to privacy regulations. The proposed methodology involved a federated learning framework where multiple edge devices or nodes collaboratively train a shared model without direct data sharing; only learned model weights are aggregated at a central server. A nonlinear model, specifically Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, was utilized within this FL framework for predicting engine faults. Using the C-MAPSS dataset, the study demonstrated the effectiveness of the FL approach in RUL prediction, aiming to optimize maintenance schedules, reduce downtime, and improve operational efficiency. The key advantages include enhanced data privacy and security, simplified regulatory compliance, reduced data transmission costs, and enabling collaboration among entities without compromising sensitive information. The main limitation identified was the complexity in managing FL across numerous devices and locations. This research addresses the critical research gap concerning privacy-preserving ML techniques in PdM for industries with sensitive operational data [4].

### 2.1.5 Prognostic and Health Management of Critical Aircraft Systems and Components: An Overview

Fu and Avdelidis (2023) provided a comprehensive overview of Prognostic and Health Management (PHM) for critical aircraft systems and components [5]. As a review paper, its objective was to analyze the current state of research advancements in prognostics, focusing on prominent algorithms, practical applications, challenges, and future directions. The authors emphasized the paramount importance of ensuring aircraft safety and reliability, noting the limitations of traditional maintenance approaches (corrective and preventive) in terms of cost, downtime, and proactive failure detection. The paper discussed various PHM methodologies, including physics-based modeling, data-driven techniques, and hybrid prognosis, and elaborated on Condition-Based Maintenance (CBM) as a strategic approach enabled by PHM. The review highlighted the benefits of PHM, such as early fault detection, proactive maintenance scheduling, data-driven decision-making, enhanced maintenance plans, and mitigation of unexpected downtime. A key research gap identified was the deficiency in research concerning the efficient integration of hybrid PHM applications, suggesting a need for more comprehensive approaches combining different modeling techniques for robust prognostics. This paper serves as a foundational text, contextualizing the importance of RUL estimation and predictive maintenance within the broader PHM framework [5].

### 2.1.6 Remaining Useful Life Prediction for Aircraft Engines using LSTM

Peringal et al. (2024) focused on predicting the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of jet engines from time-series data using a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network, comparing its performance against a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) on the NASA C-MAPSS dataset [6]. The problem statement underscored the challenges of accurate RUL prediction with traditional methods and the inadequacy of MLPs for sequential data. Their methodology involved data preprocessing with Min-Max scaling and the generation of time-series sequences for LSTM. The LSTM network was defined with specific layers and units, optimized using the Adam optimizer, and trained iteratively. The results consistently showed that the LSTM model outperformed the MLP in prediction accuracy, achieving a stable Mean Squared Error (MSE) of 796.42 compared to the MLP's 1745. This demonstrated LSTM's superior ability to capture temporal dependencies in jet engine degradation patterns. The study highlighted the advantage of sequence-aware models for time-series RUL prediction and the importance of effective data preprocessing. The research gap addressed was the need for models capable of handling the inherent temporal dynamics in engine degradation data, which simpler architectures like MLPs struggle with [6].

### 2.1.7 An Interpretable Systematic Review of Machine Learning Models for Predictive Maintenance of Aircraft Engine

Al Hasib et al. (2023) presented an interpretable systematic review of various machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) models for predictive maintenance of aircraft engines, specifically aiming to predict engine failure within a predetermined number of cycles [7]. The authors acknowledged the disastrous consequences of engine failure and the need for interpretability in AI models. Their methodology involved comparing a wide range of models, including LSTM, Bi-LSTM, RNN, Bi-RNN, GRU, Random Forest, KNN, Naive Bayes, and Gradient Boosting, on the NASA C-MAPSS dataset. Data preprocessing included RUL label creation and dimensional transformation for deep learning models to handle sequence data. Crucially, LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) was applied to understand and explain the behavior of these models, particularly why ML models often underperformed compared to DL models. The study reported high accuracies for deep learning models: GRU (97.8%), Bi-LSTM (97.14%), and LSTM (96.42%), reinforcing their effectiveness for early-stage maintenance prediction. This work is significant for its comparative analysis and, more importantly, for integrating interpretability, addressing the 'black box' nature of many AI models in safety-critical applications. The research gap highlighted was the need for interpretable predictive maintenance models and understanding their decision-making processes [7].

### 2.1.8 Parsimonious Kernel Recursive Least Squares Algorithm for Aero-Engine Health Diagnosis

Zhou et al. (2018) developed a novel kernel adaptive filtering (KAF) algorithm, the parsimonious Kernel Recursive Least Squares (PKRLS) algorithm, for aero-engine health diagnosis [8]. The objective was to address the issue of continuously growing network size and computational burden in traditional KAF methods while maintaining satisfactory prediction accuracy for real-time applications. The problem statement identified that conventional KAF methods become computationally expensive and memory-intensive as training samples accumulate, and existing sparsification techniques often compromise accuracy. The PKRLS methodology incorporated a pruning approach into the KRLS algorithm to restrict network size by removing the least important centers from the dictionary, with importance quantified by contribution to minimizing the cost function. The algorithm combined three learning strategies: coefficient updates, structure updates using the ALD criterion, and pruning when the dictionary size exceeded a preset threshold. A multi-sensor health diagnosis method based on PKRLS was developed for real-time identification of degraded aero-engine health states. Tested on an aero-engine degradation dataset, PKRLS achieved a parsimonious network structure with satisfactory prediction accuracy and fast prediction speed. The main advantages include controlled network growth, efficient pruning without significant accuracy loss, and suitability for online, real-time applications. This research addresses the research gap concerning computational efficiency and memory usage in online adaptive filtering algorithms for complex machinery [8].
